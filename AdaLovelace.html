Biography

Ada Lovelace, "Enchantress of Numbers," was the first computer programmer. Unlike most women of her time, Lovelace was raised being taught science and math. After showing a natural ability, she received teaching from many prestigious people, including Mary Somerville, one of the first women to be included in the Royal Astronomical Society.

However, Lovelace's most notable mentor was Charles Babbage, the "father of the computer." From him she was able to form her ideas about coding. She created an article from a lecture that Babbage gave and explained her original ideas, but received little recognition. These ideas turned out to be used to create a machine algorithm that calculated Bernoulli numbers. She also predicted that computers could do more than just calculate numbers. She presented the idea that they could be used to create music, show pictures, and advance scientific discoveries. Soon after, she wrote this, she died of uterine cancer at the age of 36. About 90 years after, a man named Alan Turing used her work to create The Universal Turing Machine.
